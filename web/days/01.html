Training AI to be my video editor

written · edited · captioned · published — by AI

A random account reposted my video on Instagram and got more comments than my original got likes. So I'm spending 30 days teaching an AI to do the work for me. Day 1 is the planning episode — and the plugin you can install to do this for your own series.

$ /plugin install day-01-series-planner@andrewislearning

Then ask Claude: "Help me plan a 30-day series about [your topic]." The skill walks you through five short questions and writes SERIES_PLAN.md, DAY_01_SCRIPT.md, and TOOLS.md straight into your project folder. Same questions I answered to plan this series.

What this is, in plain English

I'm a founder, not a video editor. I shoot vertical selfies on my phone like everyone else. The problem is what comes after — cutting the boring parts, fixing audio, adding captions, posting to two platforms, sending DMs to people who comment. That's where most creators spend their evenings, and it's exactly the kind of work AI is good at.

So I'm running a 30-day public experiment: teach an AI to do every step except the recording itself. By Day 30, I post a video and the only thing I do is hold the phone. Each day I add one piece. Each day's piece becomes a plugin you can install — so by the end, you'll have the same toolkit I do.

How AI made this — step by step

Six small Python scripts ran one after the other. Each one is open-source. Each one does a single thing well. None of them is more than ~150 lines of code. You don't need to understand Python to use them — Claude wrote them, and Claude can run them for you too. But here's what each one actually did for this video.

1

transcribe.py — turning audio into words

Whisper (the open-source AI speech-to-text model from OpenAI) listened to my selfie footage and gave me back a JSON file with every word I said and the exact millisecond it started and ended. Word-level timestamps are the foundation for everything else — captions, cuts, sync.

Beginner shortcut: Whisper is free and runs locally. The first time you use it, it downloads a ~250 MB model and you never need to touch it again. After that, transcribing a 50-second clip takes about 10 seconds on a regular laptop.

2

tighten.py — cutting the boring parts

I watched my own footage once and noted the timestamps of dead air, long pauses, and beats that felt redundant. Then I told tighten.py to "keep only these windows of the video" via a small JSON config. ffmpeg actually performed the cuts.

Result: a 67-second raw take became a 60-second tight take. The thing that's special is the cuts are described in JSON, not made by clicking around a timeline. You can re-do them by editing the file. No clicking, no scrubbing.

3

assemble.py — layering b-roll over the audio

The selfie audio is the spine of the video — your voice plays uninterrupted from start to finish. assemble.py swaps out the visual at scripted moments while the audio keeps going. That's why you see the IG repost screenshot, my Cowork screen, and the writing/editing/publishing scenes — they replace the visual but my voice stays continuous.

The trick: a JSON config says "at second 8, swap the visual to broll_take_01.mp4 for 6 seconds." ffmpeg does the swap. You can re-arrange b-roll just by editing numbers in the JSON.

4

caption.py — burning captions onto the video

Using the word-level timestamps from step 1, this script groups my words into 1–3-word chunks ("AI agent" stays together, "Claude plugin" stays together) and generates an ASS subtitle file — that's an advanced subtitle format that supports per-word styling. Then ffmpeg burns the captions directly into the video pixels, so they show up the same on every platform regardless of caption settings.

The salmon-coral words are emphasis (DAY 1, AI AGENT, FREE GUIDE, CLAUDE PLUGIN). The white words are everything else. Position is at the lower third — out of the way of my face but above Instagram's UI overlays.

5

finish.py — audio polish, color, and pacing

One ffmpeg pass that does three things at once:

  • Audio: high-pass filter (kills room rumble), gentle noise reduction (cleans hiss), light compression (evens out my volume), and loudness normalization to -14 LUFS — that's the loudness target Instagram and TikTok use, so my audio plays at the same volume as everyone else's. Without this, platforms re-mix your audio at upload and quality degrades.
  • Color: a tiny bump in contrast and saturation. Not heavy "Instagram filter" territory — just enough to make the colors feel intentional.
  • Speed: 1.08× playback. Just enough to feel snappy without sounding sped-up.
6

publish.py — posting to Instagram and TikTok at once

A single API call to Post for Me that uploads the video and publishes it to my Instagram Reel and TikTok account simultaneously. ~30 seconds from local file to live post on both platforms.

No "save to camera roll, switch to IG app, swipe up, paste caption, scroll to find a cover frame, repeat for TikTok." That whole 5-minute manual flow is one curl request now.

If you've never written code before

Here's how to think about this without getting overwhelmed:

  1. Each step is a small tool. Each one does ONE thing. You can run them one at a time, in order.
  2. The tools chain together. The output of step 1 becomes the input to step 2, and so on. The whole pipeline is just files in folders.
  3. Claude wrote almost all of this. When you install the plugin, you can ask Claude to plan, build, or fix any of these steps for your own video. You're not writing code — you're directing.
  4. Start with the planning step. Install the plugin (one command), tell Claude what your series is about, and let it build the plan. That's Day 1's deliverable. Days 2–30 add the rest.
