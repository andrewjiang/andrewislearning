# Short-Form Reels — Best Practices Rubric

This is the knowledge base the `review-script` skill applies. Every claim
has a citation. Update as new research lands; do not invent rules.

---

## 1. The first 3 seconds

The single highest-leverage section of the script.

- Up to **50% of viewers drop off in the first three seconds.** Reels with
  3-second hold above 60% outperform those below 40% by **5–10× in total
  reach**. Source: [Digital Influence — Reels Hook research](https://www.getdigitalinfluence.com/marketing-tips/instagram-reels-hooks).
- Reels with a **storytelling hook or jump cut in the first 3 seconds**
  are **72% more likely to go viral**. Source: [OpusClip — Hook Formulas](https://www.opus.pro/blog/instagram-reels-hook-formulas).
- The 3-second hold is **one of the most important early algorithmic
  signals** Instagram uses to decide whether to push to Explore.

### What kills the hook
- **Slow-build openers.** "Hey guys, welcome back…", "So today I want to
  talk about…" — these are explicitly called out as "death sentences for
  retention." Cut anything that is *throat-clearing* before the actual
  content.
- **Vague claims.** "I have something amazing to share" beats nothing,
  but "My X video got a million views and got reposted to Instagram"
  beats it 10:1 because of specificity.
- **Static talking head with no visual change.** Pattern interrupts in
  the first 3 seconds (hard cut, big text reveal, sudden zoom) materially
  improve hold rates.

### What works
- **Disaster / open-loop hooks** that promise a payoff: *"Yesterday I
  almost didn't make it to Day 2."* Viewer must keep watching to find
  out what happened.
- **Specific numbers in the hook** ground the claim: *"My video got 950k
  views. Someone reposted it. They got more comments than I got likes."*
- **Visual hooks** — text overlays, motion graphics, jump cuts — landing
  inside the first 1–2 seconds. Bigger and bolder than feels comfortable
  is usually right.

### Rule of thumb
Script the first 3 seconds **word-for-word**, then outline 3–5 key
beats for the remainder. The first 3 seconds are not where you improvise.

## 2. Length

- **60–90 seconds is the engagement sweet spot** on Instagram Reels.
  Source: [OpusClip — Ideal Reel length](https://www.opus.pro/blog/ideal-instagram-reels-length).
- Under 90 seconds retains roughly **50% of viewers on average**. After
  90s, completion drops noticeably. After 2 minutes, views decline
  consistently.
- 33% of marketers consider 31–60 seconds optimal for short-form;
  60–90s is the recommended window when the content has substance
  (lead generation, education, demonstration). Source: [Yaguara — Short-Form Stats](https://www.yaguara.co/short-form-video-statistics/).

### Rule of thumb
Target **45–60 seconds** for personal/series content where the same
viewer returns daily. Use the longer end (75–90s) only when there is
genuine narrative substance that earns the extra time. Never pad to
hit a target length.

## 3. Three-act story arc

Short-form videos that retain well follow a compressed three-act
structure. Source: [Influencers Time — 60-Second Story Arc Briefs](https://www.influencers-time.com/60-second-story-arc-briefs-that-boost-completion-rates/).

| Act | Window | Job |
|-----|--------|-----|
| I — Hook | 0–8s | Open loop, set stakes, promise the payoff |
| II — Tension | 8–45s | Escalate the problem, weave in the substance |
| III — Payoff | 45–60s | Resolve the loop, deliver the value, single CTA |

The **problem → solution → result** mapping is a specific instance of
this:
- Problem stated in first 10s
- Solution shown in middle 40s
- Result demonstrated in final 10s

### Where scripts go wrong on structure
- **Burying the payoff** past 50s. Viewers who made it that far deserve
  the resolution.
- **Front-loading recap** in a series. Returning viewers don't need it
  every time; new viewers can pick it up from context. Long recaps
  (>10s) at the start of an episode are *especially* expensive because
  they compete with the hook window for attention.
- **No open loop.** If the first 3 seconds don't pose a question or
  promise something, there's nothing pulling the viewer forward.

## 4. Captions

- **Burned-in subtitles perform 2.5–3.4× better than auto-captions or
  no captions.** Source: [OpusClip — Caption Best Practices](https://www.opus.pro/blog/instagram-reels-caption-subtitle-best-practices).
- Captioned Reels get **25% more shares** than uncaptioned ones
  (Instagram's own data, per OpusClip).
- Animated captions report **15–40% higher retention** than static.
- Captions should occupy approximately **30% of the screen** at peak.
- Animation style matters: **simple fade-in or gentle pop** wins.
  Over-styled / aggressive animations make the Reel feel amateurish and
  reduce watch time.

### Caption rules
- 1–3 word chunks. Multi-word phrases (proper nouns, brand terms) stay
  together as atomic chunks.
- Bold weight. High contrast against any background — usually white text
  with thick black outline, or pill background.
- Keyword emphasis: a different color (and slightly larger size) on
  load-bearing words. Do not exceed ~20% of words emphasized.
- Captions in lower-third clear of platform UI (bottom ~14% reserved for
  IG controls).

## 5. CTA

A single, specific, keyword-based CTA is the highest-converting pattern.

- **Keyword-triggered DM funnels convert at 5–15%** vs 1–3% for
  "link in bio". Source: [CommuniPass — Auto-DM strategies](https://communipass.com/blog/how-coaches-use-instagram-auto-dm-to-sell-digital-products-not-just-collect-leads/), [InstantDM — DM automation](https://instantdm.com/blog/instagram-dm-automation-for-lead-generation-2026).
- A 100k-view Reel generating 1k keyword comments creates 1k DM contacts
  *simultaneously*. Comment-to-DM removes friction; "navigate to bio,
  click link" loses most viewers.
- **Multiple CTAs reduce conversion.** Pick one goal per Reel.

### Keyword discipline
- **Short, easy to spell:** LINK, GUIDE, FREE, SEND, REVIEW, BOOK.
- **Specific to the offer:** if the deliverable is a recipe, "RECIPE"
  outperforms "FREE". Words that match the asset name stick better.
- **Test alternatives.** "GUIDE" might outperform "FREE" for a given
  audience; the only way to know is to A/B at scale.
- **Single character / number works less well** because comments often
  get filtered as spam.

### Phrasing
*"Comment '[KEYWORD]' and I'll send you the [thing]."* is the
template that consistently converts highest. Variations:
- *"Comment '[KEYWORD]' and I'll DM you the link."*
- *"Want it? Comment '[KEYWORD]'."*
- Avoid *"Save for later"* and *"Follow for more"* as the *primary*
  CTA — they are warm-up CTAs, not conversion CTAs.

## 6. Pacing & cuts

- **Average shot length 1.5–3 seconds** for high-energy short form.
- A static talking-head shot longer than 8 seconds is a retention risk
  unless the audio is genuinely compelling.
- Visual variation every 3–4 seconds: cut to b-roll, zoom-punch on the
  speaker, text card, or graphics overlay.

### Specific to a selfie + b-roll format
- B-roll inserts should appear within ~0.5 seconds of the words they
  illustrate. A 2-second delay between *"reposted to Instagram"* and the
  Instagram b-roll feels disconnected.
- Concrete b-roll (the actual artifact, screenshot, screen recording)
  beats stock footage of related concepts every time.
- Zoom-punches (small 100→106% scale over 200–300ms) on emphasis words
  break up static shots cheaply.

## 7. Voice & specificity

Across all the research, one rule appears repeatedly: **specificity
beats abstraction**. This is not a style preference; it is correlated
with retention and shares.

### Strong (specific)
- "My X video got nearly a million views"
- "The post went up totally black for nine hours"
- "Shoutout to Kevin for the one like on the black video"
- "Day 1 used Whisper, ffmpeg, Claude Code, Post for Me"

### Weak (vague)
- "My video did well online"
- "There were some issues with the post"
- "Thanks to my friends for the support"
- "I used a bunch of AI tools"

The weak versions are *cheaper to write* but they're forgettable. The
specific version takes 2 extra seconds and 3 extra words but creates
a quotable moment, a screenshot-able beat, or a specific image in the
viewer's head.

### Self-deprecating beats
A self-aware moment ("I almost didn't make it") earns trust and softens
authority. Use sparingly — too much and the speaker reads as anxious or
hedging. Once per video, max.

## 8. The rubric — running checklist

When reviewing a script, work through these in order:

1. **First 3 seconds** — does it open a loop? Is there a pattern
   interrupt? Is it specific?
2. **Length** — between 45 and 90s after speedup? If longer, does the
   content earn it?
3. **Three-act structure** — hook → tension → payoff. Mark which
   sentences belong to which act.
4. **Recap discipline** — is recap longer than 6 seconds at the top?
   Can it be trimmed or moved later?
5. **Captions plan** — are the captions burned-in, animated, with
   keyword emphasis? Is the screen-share calculated (~30%)?
6. **CTA** — single? Keyword-based? Specific to the offer?
7. **Pacing** — average shot length under 6s? Any static stretch over
   8s? B-roll synced within 0.5s of the relevant words?
8. **Specificity audit** — find every vague phrase and propose a
   specific replacement.
9. **Self-deprecation budget** — at most one self-aware beat.
10. **Open loops resolved** — every promise made in the hook gets paid
    off by the end.

For each finding, output:
- **Severity:** blocker / improvement / nit
- **Reference:** shot or timestamp
- **Diagnosis:** one sentence
- **Fix:** the proposed line, or the proposed cut
